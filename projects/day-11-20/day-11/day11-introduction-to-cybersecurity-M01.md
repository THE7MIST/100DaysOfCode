# Module 1: Introduction to Cybersecurity

## 1.1 The World of Cybersecurity

### Introduction

Welcome to the world of cybersecurity.

In this module, Guru from eLearning Company @Apollo introduces the importance of cybersecurity and explains how individuals, organizations, and governments must protect themselves from cyber threats.

Cybersecurity has become essential because cybercriminals continuously attempt to steal money, personal information, identities, and sensitive organizational data.

---

# 1.1.1 What Is Cybersecurity?

## Definition

Cybersecurity is the ongoing effort to protect individuals, organizations, and governments from digital attacks by protecting networked systems and data from unauthorized access, misuse, damage, or theft.

Cybersecurity focuses on three levels of protection:

### 1. Individual Protection

Protecting personal information, devices, accounts, and digital identity.

Examples:

- Password security
- Multi-factor authentication
- Privacy protection
- Secure online behavior

### 2. Organizational Protection

Protecting company systems, employees, applications, and business data.

Examples:

- Firewalls
- Intrusion Detection Systems
- Security Monitoring
- Access Control

### 3. Government Protection

Protecting national infrastructure and sensitive information.

Examples:

- Military systems
- Public services
- National databases
- Critical infrastructure

---

# 1.1.2 Protecting Your Personal Data

## What Is Personal Data?

Personal data is any information that can identify an individual.

Examples:

- Full Name
- Address
- Age
- Phone Number
- Date of Birth
- Email Address
- Social Media Profiles

Personal data exists in both offline and online forms.

---

## Offline Identity

Your offline identity is the real-life identity you present every day.

Examples:

- Name
- Address
- School Information
- Workplace Information
- Family Details

### Risks

Identity thieves may obtain information through:

- Stolen documents
- Lost wallets
- Physical observation
- Social engineering

---

## Online Identity

Your online identity represents who you are on the internet.

Examples:

- Usernames
- Email addresses
- Social media profiles
- Gaming accounts
- Online communities

### Important Note

Even if you do not use social media, you still have an online identity whenever you use the internet.

### Best Practice

Share as little personal information as possible online.

---

# 1.1.3 Your Online Identity

## Choosing a Secure Username

### Good Examples

- jdoe.IT
- j.doe12

### Poor Examples

- jdoe
- jdoe1990
- jane.doe

### Why?

Avoid usernames that reveal:

- Full names
- Birth years
- Personal information

Cybercriminals often use publicly available information during attacks.

# 1.1.5 Where Is Your Data?

Many people assume that information shared with friends remains private.

This is often incorrect.

---

## Example Scenario

### Step 1

You take photos on your smartphone.

Copies exist on:

- Mobile device
- Cloud backup systems

### Step 2

You share photos with five friends.

Copies now exist on:

- Your device
- Their devices

### Step 3

One friend uploads the photos online.

Copies now exist on:

- Social media servers
- Cloud platforms
- Backup systems
- Unknown third-party systems

### Result

People you have never met may now have access to your photos.

---

# 1.1.6 What's More...

Every time we collect or share personal data, we should consider security implications.

---

## Medical Records Example

After a doctor's appointment:

- Medical records are updated
- Information may be shared with insurance companies
- Additional organizations gain access to sensitive information

### Risk

More copies of data increase exposure.

---

## Store Loyalty Card Example

Many stores offer loyalty programs.

Benefits:

- Discounts
- Rewards
- Special offers

Hidden Cost:

Stores build detailed customer profiles based on:

- Purchases
- Preferences
- Shopping habits

This information may be shared with marketing partners.

# 1.1.8 What Do Hackers Want?

The primary goal of many cybercriminals is financial gain.

However, money is not the only target.

---

## Example: Phishing Attack

A victim receives an email appearing to be from a bank.

The email asks them to verify account details.

A link directs the victim to a fake website.

If the victim enters:

- Username
- Password
- Banking information

The attacker can use that information to:

- Access bank accounts
- Transfer funds
- Commit fraud

---

## Other Cybercriminal Goals

- Identity Theft
- Data Theft
- Extortion
- Financial Fraud
- Account Takeover

---

# 1.1.9 Identity Theft

Identity theft occurs when someone steals personal information and uses it as their own.

---

## Medical Identity Theft

Cybercriminals steal:

- Medical insurance information
- Healthcare credentials

Consequences:

- Fraudulent treatments
- Incorrect medical records
- Financial losses

---

## Banking Identity Theft

Attackers use stolen information to access:

- Bank accounts
- Credit cards
- Social media accounts
- Financial services

Potential consequences:

- Unauthorized loans
- Fake tax returns
- Damaged credit ratings
- Financial losses

---

# 1.1.10 Who Else Wants My Data?

Cybercriminals are not the only entities interested in personal data.

---

## Internet Service Providers (ISPs)

ISPs can track:

- Websites visited
- Internet activity
- Usage patterns

In some regions they may:

- Sell data to advertisers
- Share information with government agencies

---

## Advertisers

Advertisers monitor:

- Shopping habits
- Preferences
- Browsing behavior

Purpose:

- Deliver targeted advertisements

---

## Search Engines and Social Media Platforms

These platforms may collect:

- Gender
- Location
- Phone numbers
- Search history
- Interests
- Behavioral data

This information is often used for advertising and analytics.

---

## Websites

Many websites use cookies.

### Purpose

- Personalization
- Analytics
- Tracking user behavior

### Risk

Cookies create a digital trail linked to your online identity.
